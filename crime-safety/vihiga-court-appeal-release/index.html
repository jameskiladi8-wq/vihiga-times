Court of Appeal Quashes Death Sentences, Frees Two Men Over Procedural Flaw

The Court of Appeal overturned convictions and death sentences for two men convicted in a 2009 Kivagala robbery, citing a procedural error involving a judge's jurisdiction. The men have been ordered released.

2 June 2026 8 min read By Vihiga Times Reporter Updated 12:00 PM

The Court of Appeal this week set aside convictions and death sentences handed to two men over a 2009 robbery in Kivagala, Vihiga County, ordering their immediate release. Benard Ingunza and David Libabu had been convicted by a magistrate's court and later had their sentences upheld by the High Court in Kakamega in 2013. The original case stemmed from an attack on a woman in her home, during which household items, livestock and KSh22,450 in cash were reported stolen and the complainant said she was assaulted.

On appeal, judges found serious procedural and jurisdictional defects in the earlier proceedings. Most notably, one judge who took part in the previous hearings was serving in the Employment and Labour Relations Court—a bench that has no remit to hear criminal matters. That irregularity, among others identified by the appellate bench, undermined the legality of the conviction, prompting the Court of Appeal to quash the verdicts and free the two men.

Official Response

Court records show the appellate bench reviewed trial records, witness statements and the composition of the benches that heard the case. Officials at the Court of Appeal issued the orders to set aside the convictions and remove the death sentences. Authorities had not provided further comment on whether a retrial would be sought by the prosecution at the time of publication. [Official Statement Link Placeholder]

The Office of the Director of Public Prosecutions and Kakamega High Court registry had not yet issued public responses when we contacted them. Local police confirmed they had received the court orders and would act on them immediately. Family members of the accused greeted the development with relief, while the complainant and some community members said they were still processing the news.

Why This Matters

The ruling is important on several levels. First, it underscores how courtroom procedures and judicial composition can determine the outcome of serious criminal cases. When a judge without criminal jurisdiction participates in a trial, the integrity of that trial is open to challenge, regardless of the evidence presented.

Consequences of the Jurisdictional Error
  • Two men freed after years under death sentence.
  • Questions raised about court roster oversight and judicial assignments.
  • Complainant and community left without resolution on the original crime.
  • Renewed calls for legal aid and victim support services in Vihiga.

Second, the case raises wider concerns in Vihiga and neighboring counties about oversight and the protection of defendants' rights. For victims and communities, the decision revives difficult questions: how to balance the need for finality in criminal cases with the legal safeguards that ensure trials are fair and lawful. For the accused, who spent years under the shadow of a death sentence, the ruling restores liberty but leaves unresolved questions about responsibility for the delay and the psychological toll of prolonged litigation.

Community Impact or Background Context

Kivagala residents and others across Vihiga have followed the case with interest since the original conviction. Reactions were mixed: while some neighbours and civil-society observers welcomed the correction of a legal irregularity, others worried that the decision might leave victims feeling that justice was unfinished.

This is not the first time procedural flaws have affected long-running criminal cases in western Kenya. Legal experts say such rulings often prompt audits of court rosters and reminders about strict compliance with jurisdictional limits. Meanwhile, community groups in Vihiga have renewed calls for better legal aid access and clearer communication between courts, police and victims' support services to reduce the human cost when retrials or appeals disrupt closure.

Some community elders say the ruling should force a reckoning about how courts manage roster assignments and communicate with affected communities. They argue that judicial administrators should treat bench composition as a priority check, not just an administrative formality, to prevent similar reversals.

Youth groups and legal aid associations in the area have also called for better record-keeping and early reporting of jurisdictional concerns, saying that many procedural errors are only caught after years of litigation. They say cooperation between courts, police and community paralegals is essential if the justice system is to become more reliable.

Safety & Prevention

For citizens, the case highlights the importance of preserving records and reporting concerns early in the court process. Victims should be made aware of their rights, including how to follow up on appeals and to access legal support. Courts and judicial administrators must ensure hearings are assigned to appropriately mandated benches to prevent similar miscarriages.

Protecting Rights in the Justice System
  • Ensure criminal cases are heard only by properly mandated benches.
  • Report jurisdictional concerns to court registrars early in proceedings.
  • Preserve all trial records and witness statements for appeal review.
  • Expand legal aid access for defendants and victims in rural counties.

Legal aid organisations and county victim support services can play a role by offering counselling and clear explanations of next steps when rulings change. Where community trust erodes after a high-profile legal reversal, local leaders and civil society should prioritise outreach to both victims and released suspects to reduce tensions.

What Happens Next

The prosecution may consider whether to seek a retrial; the Court of Appeal's order does not necessarily preclude further lawful proceedings if they correct the procedural errors. Police will comply with the release order, and families of both parties will likely await word from the Director of Public Prosecutions. Community leaders have called for transparent updates and for authorities to brief victims on available remedies and support. Details remain developing, and officials have not yet announced whether a retrial or further appeals will follow. [Official Statement Link Placeholder]

Details remain developing and readers should expect updates as investigators and court officials provide further information. For now, the community waits for answers — about whether a retrial will proceed, how the complainant will be supported, and whether this ruling will finally trigger the roster audits and procedural reforms legal experts have been demanding for years.
